Our very idea of productivity is premised on the idea of producing something new, whereas we do not tend to class maintenance and care as productive in the same way. To do nothing is to hold yourself in a state of receptivity, to listen, to look, and to feel. It is a way of being that is not about the self, but about the world. It is a way of being that is not about the future, but about the present.

In a world where our value is determined by our productivity, many of us find ourselves training our minds to think like a machine. We want to be efficient, we want to be fast, and we want to be able to multitask. But the mind is not a machine. The mind is a living thing, and it needs time to rest, to wander, and to just be.

The 'nothing' that I am talking about is nothing from the point of view of the attention economy. It is a 'nothing' that is actually a 'something'—a something that is vital to our health, our happiness, and our ability to think clearly. It is the 'nothing' of sitting in a park and watching the birds, the 'nothing' of a long conversation with a friend, the 'nothing' of a quiet afternoon spent reading a book. These things are not 'productive' in the sense that they do not produce a commodity that can be bought or sold. But they are 'productive' in the sense that they produce a life that is worth living.
